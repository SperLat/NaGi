# Nagi Product Principles
**From Kasvu to product — the cultivation layer**

These principles are the design constitution for Nagi. When a feature decision is unclear, return here first.

---

## 1. Cultivate confidence, don't transfer knowledge

The goal is not to teach elders about technology. It is to create conditions where their natural curiosity can surface and their confidence can grow from within.

Instruction breeds dependency. Confidence breeds agency.

**In practice:** every interaction is designed so the elder experiences success, not receives explanation. The learning happens inside the doing, not before it.

---

## 2. The intermediary is the gardener, not the teacher

The intermediary's role is to prepare the conditions — language, context, familiar contacts, a trusted environment — and then step back. Nagi amplifies what the intermediary already does naturally; it does not replace their judgment or their relationship.

**In practice:** the intermediary configures once; the elder uses freely. Remote adjustment is possible, but the elder's experience is never interrupted or corrected mid-session.

---

## 3. Blame the system, not the elder

When an elder cannot complete a task, the correct question is: *what in the design made this hard?* Not: *why can't they do this?*

Error states are design failures. Recovery is designed in from the beginning — not apologized for after the fact.

**In practice:** every error state has an explicit recovery path. "I'm stuck" is always reachable. The app never dead-ends.

---

## 4. One action per screen, one purpose per moment

Cognitive clarity is not about simplification for its own sake. It is about respect for how attention works. Every screen has one clear invitation. This is how trust builds across interactions — each successful step is evidence for the next.

**In practice:** the elder's home screen has exactly 4 cards. No more without explicit intermediary configuration. The intermediary dashboard has a clear primary action. Density is an anti-feature here.

---

## 5. Trust before confidence, connection before capability

Elders adopt technology when someone they trust is visibly present in the experience. The feeling of accompaniment — that someone set this up for you, that someone will notice if you're stuck — is more powerful than any UX optimization.

**In practice:** the elder's interface prominently surfaces who configured it and who to call. The intermediary sees activity. Presence is designed in.

---

## 6. The echo effect

One elder who succeeds in using technology with dignity becomes living evidence for another. Confidence spreads through community — through stories, through visible use, through a neighbor who figured out video calls and shows you how.

**In practice:** design for sharable success. Progress is visible to the intermediary, who becomes the carrier of the story. A family member who sees their grandmother using the app confidently tells their neighbor.

---

## On frugality and scope

Nagi is not a super-app. Every feature added is a cost to the elder's clarity. The product earns the right to add complexity only after the existing surface has been proven to build confidence — not before.

When in doubt: remove, not add.
